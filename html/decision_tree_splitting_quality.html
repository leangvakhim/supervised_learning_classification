Decision Tree Splitting Quality

Evaluating Information Gain, Split Information, and Gain Ratio

The Classroom Dilemma

Imagine you are trying to predict whether a student will Pass or Fail a final exam. You have a dataset of 15 students.

  • ✅ 9 Students Passed
  • ❌ 6 Students Failed

You are building a Decision Tree, and you need to choose the best feature to split your data. You have two choices:

Choice A: Attendance

Splits the class into two groups: High or Low attendance. Makes logical sense.

Choice B: Student ID

Splits the class into 15 groups based on their unique ID number. Every group gets exactly 1 student.

Which split is mathematically better? Let's prove it step-by-step.

🧪

Step 0: Measuring the "Messiness"

Before we split anything, we need to know how impure (messy) our starting classroom is. We measure this using Entropy ($E$).

Entropy Formula:

$$ E = - \sum_{i=1}^{c} p_i \cdot \log_2(p_i) $$

Where $p$ is the probability of a specific class (Pass or Fail).

Let's calculate the Entropy for our starting class of 15 students (9 Pass, 6 Fail):

$$ E_{start} = - \left( \frac{9}{15} \log_2 \left(\frac{9}{15}\right) + \frac{6}{15} \log_2 \left(\frac{6}{15}\right) \right) $$
$$ E_{start} \approx 0.971 $$

What does this mean? Entropy goes from 0 (perfectly pure) to 1.0 (perfectly mixed). A score of 0.971 means our classroom is highly mixed and uncertain.

📈

Step 1: Information Gain (IG)

Information Gain measures how much impurity we removed by splitting the data.

$$ IG = E_{start} - E_{new\_weighted} $$

Split A: Attendance

1. Group High (10 students: 8 Pass, 2 Fail)

$$ E_{high} = -[0.8\log_2(0.8) + 0.2\log_2(0.2)] \approx 0.722 $$

2. Group Low (5 students: 1 Pass, 4 Fail)

$$ E_{low} = -[0.2\log_2(0.2) + 0.8\log_2(0.8)] \approx 0.722 $$

3. Weighted Entropy

Multiply each entropy by the group's size ($10/15$ and $5/15$):

$$ E_{weighted} = \left(\frac{10}{15} \times 0.722\right) + \left(\frac{5}{15} \times 0.722\right) = 0.722 $$
Final Calculation $$ IG = 0.971 - 0.722 $$ IG = 0.249

Split B: Student ID

1. The Groups (15 groups of 1 student)

Each group is 100% pure (only 1 outcome possible).

$$ E_{each} = -[1\log_2(1) + 0] = 0 $$

2. Weighted Entropy

Since every single group has an entropy of 0:

$$ E_{weighted} = \sum_{i=1}^{15} \left(\frac{1}{15} \times 0\right) = 0 $$
Final Calculation $$ IG = 0.971 - 0 $$ IG = 0.971
The Trap! If we only look at Information Gain, the Decision Tree will pick Student ID because it looks "perfect". But we know ID is useless for future predictions!
⚖️

Step 2: Split Information (SI)

To stop the tree from cheating, we use Split Information. This acts as a mathematical penalty for chopping the data into too many tiny pieces.

Split Information Formula:

$$ SI = - \sum \left( \frac{|Group|}{|Total|} \cdot \log_2 \left(\frac{|Group|}{|Total|}\right) \right) $$

Split A: Attendance

Just 2 groups (10/15 and 5/15).

$$ - \left( \frac{10}{15}\log_2(\frac{10}{15}) + \frac{5}{15}\log_2(\frac{5}{15}) \right) $$ SI ≈ 0.918
(Low Penalty)

Split B: Student ID

15 groups (1/15 each).

$$ - \sum_{i=1}^{15} \left( \frac{1}{15}\log_2(\frac{1}{15}) \right) $$ SI ≈ 3.907
(MASSIVE Penalty!)
🏆

Step 3: Gain Ratio (GR)

Finally, we calculate the Gain Ratio. It balances the purity gained against the penalty of fragmentation. This is the ultimate decider.

$$ Gain\ Ratio = \frac{Information\ Gain\ (IG)}{Split\ Information\ (SI)} $$

⭐ Split A: Attendance (Winner)

Good purity, very low penalty.

$$ GR = \frac{0.249}{0.918} $$ GR ≈ 0.271

Split B: Student ID (Rejected)

Perfect purity destroyed by massive penalty.

$$ GR = \frac{0.971}{3.907} $$ GR ≈ 0.248
💡

Conclusion & "What If?"

The TL;DR Summary

  • 🎯 Information Gain: "How pure did the groups get?"
  • ⚠️ Split Information: "How aggressively did you chop the data to get that purity?"
  • ⚖️ Gain Ratio: "Was the purity actually worth the chopping?"

What if we didn't use Gain Ratio?

If we only relied on Information Gain, our model would have chosen Student ID. This causes a major Machine Learning problem called Overfitting.

🚨

The Overfitting Disaster

The tree would memorize the training data. If Student ID #4 passed, the tree learns "ID 4 = Pass."

But what happens next semester when a brand new student arrives with Student ID #16? The tree crashes! It has never seen ID #16 and has no idea what to do. It memorized the past instead of learning the underlying patterns (like Attendance).

By using Gain Ratio, we build smarter, more generalized AI!
